What Sal experiences

An AI conversation, not a dashboard

Receives an AI-composed SMS shift summary with individual employee names at shift start. The agent adapts the message based on what's happening ‚Äî a clean shift reads differently from a shift with 3 NCNS. Sal replies in natural language to confirm or correct the classification and consequences of each exception ‚Äî not the attendance fact itself, but whether it's "tardy, points apply" or "excused, no points." When confirming a gap, the agent asks how Sal wants to handle coverage ‚Äî moving someone on-site (the most common path) or texting the available list for external help.

Total interaction: ~2 minutes.

Cross-Cutting Opportunity

The 15-minute window
nobody is capturing

During shift changes, outgoing and incoming supervisors overlap for roughly 15 minutes. This is the most information-rich moment of the day ‚Äî and today it's entirely verbal, unstructured, and lost.

Today

Verbal handoff, nothing captured

The outgoing supervisor walks the incoming supervisor through what happened ‚Äî who left early, which machine had issues, who's on light duty, what coverage decisions were made. It happens in a hallway conversation or quick walkthrough.

The incoming supervisor absorbs what they can, forgets half of it, and starts their own triage from scratch. No record. No structured data. Critical operational context evaporates between shifts.

With TeamSense

Structured handoff, data captured

TeamSense composes a shift handoff summary for the outgoing supervisor: "Here's what happened on your shift ‚Äî confirm or correct before you go." The outgoing supervisor reviews and validates ‚Äî adding context the system can't see ("Kevin left early but he'll be back tomorrow," "Machine 3 was down for 20 min").

The incoming supervisor receives a structured incoming brief: confirmed attendance outcomes, operational decisions, contextual notes from the outgoing supervisor ‚Äî before they even walk onto the floor.

"That first 15 minutes before or after the shift is the most critical time ‚Äî that's when supervisors need the right information to manage employees and costs."

Continuity

Operational context carries across shift boundaries instead of being lost

Data

Handoff decisions become structured records ‚Äî feeding TIP analytics and predictive models

Habit

Extends the daily conversation from shift start to shift end ‚Äî two touchpoints, not one

Strategic value: Shift handoff turns TeamSense from a shift-start tool into a shift-lifecycle tool. The outgoing supervisor's confirmation closes the loop on their shift. The incoming supervisor's brief eliminates their own cold-start triage. Every handoff creates a structured record that was previously invisible ‚Äî feeding operational intelligence, trend analysis, and cross-shift continuity that no HRIS captures.

Prerequisite for Stage 2

Building the Skills Matrix
from the ground up

Skills data doesn't live in any HRIS. It lives in messy spreadsheets, supervisors' heads, and employees' own knowledge. No integration can solve this ‚Äî we have to build the data layer ourselves.

The challenge

Every manufacturing plant has some version of a skills matrix ‚Äî often a spreadsheet on a shared drive, maintained by one person, updated inconsistently. Column names vary. Formats are messy. Certifications expire. New employees get added late. The data exists, but it's trapped in formats no system can reliably consume. And the people who know the real skills ‚Äî supervisors and employees themselves ‚Äî have no way to contribute or correct.

STEP 1

AI ingestion of messy data

Customer uploads their existing spreadsheet ‚Äî whatever format it's in. AI reads, extracts, normalizes, and maps skills to a canonical matrix. Handles inconsistent column names, merged cells, abbreviations, and mixed formats.

Similar to the AI-powered policy import in Layer 7 ‚Äî upload a messy document, get structured data back.
STEP 2

Frontline self-reporting

Employees report and correct their own skills via SMS conversation. "What machines can you operate?" "Do you have your forklift cert?" Natural language ‚Äî the same conversational model as the attendance experience.

Employees know their own skills best. Give them a channel to contribute ‚Äî and the data gets better over time.
STEP 3

Supervisor validation & correction

Supervisors validate skills as they make coverage assignments. When Sal picks Lisa to cover Line A, the system learns Lisa is capable on Line A. When Sal overrides a recommendation, the system learns what the matrix got wrong.

Every coverage decision is implicit skills validation. The matrix improves passively from operational use.

The Skills Flywheel

Day 1

AI ingests the customer's existing spreadsheet. Imperfect but functional. Coverage recommendations are availability-only.

Week 2

Employees have self-reported. Supervisors have made coverage decisions. Matrix is filling in. Recommendations start including skills.

Month 3

Matrix is operational-quality. Coverage recommendations are ranked by skills + seniority + acceptance patterns. Supervisors trust the suggestions.

Ongoing

Every coverage assignment, every correction, every new cert ‚Äî the matrix gets better. It becomes the canonical skills record that no other system has.

Why this matters: Without a skills matrix, Stage 2 recommendations are "here's who's available." With a skills matrix, they become "here's who's available and qualified." That's the difference between a text blast and an intelligent staffing system. And because the data is built through use ‚Äî not through a one-time import ‚Äî it stays current in a way that static spreadsheets never do.
